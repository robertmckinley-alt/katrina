DNA in 2 minutes

Before the genes, here's the whole idea in a few sentences.

DNA is a giant instruction book tucked inside almost every cell of the body. It is written with just four letters — A, T, C and G. A gene is one "recipe" in that book (a recipe for building or running some tiny part of you).

You carry two copies of most genes — one from each parent. A SNP (say it "snip") is a single spot where people's letters can differ — like one word being spelled slightly differently between two copies of the same book. Those tiny spelling differences are what make every person unique, and a handful of them gently nudge how the body works.

Because there are two copies, each result is shown as two letters — one per copy:

  • Homozygous means both letters match (same-same), like A A.
  • Heterozygous means the two letters differ (a mix), like A G.

That's it. The rest of this guide is just translating Katrina's letters into plain English.

The headline gene: COMT

Gene · COMT  |  marker rs4680

The body's "clean-up crew" for stress chemicals

Worth knowing
Her result: AA "Met / Met" — the slow-working version

COMT is an enzyme — a tiny worker — whose job is to clear away certain "fast" chemicals once they've done their job. The chemicals it sweeps up include adrenaline and noradrenaline (the racing-heart, sweaty, "wired" chemicals), dopamine, and the leftover pieces the body makes when it breaks down estrogen.

Katrina has the slow-working version of COMT (both copies read "A", nicknamed Met/Met). About 1 in 4 people have this. It is not a bad thing — people with the slow type often have excellent focus and memory when life is calm. But it means stress chemicals and estrogen leftovers hang around a little longer before they're cleared.

How this fits her history This lines up strikingly with Katrina's own 5.5-year diary. Again and again she describes mornings around 8:30–9:30 with a racing or pounding heart, sweating, feeling "wired," shaky and anxious — almost exactly the pattern you'd expect when adrenaline-type chemicals are slow to switch off. Her genes did not cause those mornings, but the slow COMT may help explain why her body feels them so strongly. Slow COMT is also one of the more consistently studied genes in pain sensitivity and migraine — like a "volume knob" turned up a notch for both stress chemistry and pain.

Folate & the MTHFR gene

Gene · MTHFR  |  markers rs1801133 + rs1801131

A gene that helps the body use folate (vitamin B9)

Worth knowing
C677T: AG one changed copy (heterozygous)
A1298C: GT one changed copy (heterozygous)

MTHFR is a famous little gene. Its job is one specific step in turning the folate you eat (vitamin B9, from leafy greens, beans and so on) into the active form the body actually uses. That active folate powers methylation — a constant background process that helps run DNA, brain chemicals, and the clean-up of a substance called homocysteine.

MTHFR has two very well-known variations, and Katrina carries one copy of each (called "combined heterozygous"). This is common — very roughly 1 in 5 people. The practical effect: her MTHFR enzyme runs at a gentler pace — somewhere around 60–70% of full speed. For most people that matters very little, as long as they get enough folate and B vitamins.

How this fits her history MTHFR is also one of the genes researchers have studied in migraine — especially migraine with aura (the kind with extra sensations like numbness or vertigo, which appear in Katrina's notes). Carrying just one copy of each variant is only a small nudge, not a cause. It's mentioned here because B vitamins (B2/riboflavin, B6, B12 and folate) are an active and hopeful area of migraine research.

Two related "helper" genes — MTR and MTRR, which work alongside MTHFR using vitamin B12 — were also checked. MTR is the common type; MTRR is a mix. Nothing unusual.

The genetics of migraine

This is the part Katrina's family will care about most — so it's worth being very clear and very honest.

Migraine is what scientists call polygenic. That means it is not one gene. It is the combined whisper of 100+ genes, each adding a tiny amount, mixed together with hormones, sleep, stress, weather and more. There is no single "migraine gene" to find.

We looked at four of the most-studied migraine-linked genes — TRPM8, LRP1, PRDM16 and MTDH. Katrina carries the common version at some and the migraine-associated version at others — a completely ordinary mix that millions of people share. None of these "causes" her migraines.

The honest takeaway The genes most meaningfully connected to her picture are the two we already met — COMT (pain and stress chemistry) and MTHFR. But the loudest signal of all isn't in her DNA file at all — it's in her own diary. Across 5.5 years she repeatedly ties her flare-ups to her hormones, and even tracked her blood estrogen against her symptoms. That makes her history the single best "test" anyone could have. The way to read it: genes set the stage; hormones, environment and daily life direct the play.

Hormones & estrogen

Genes · ESR1 + CYP1B1 (+ COMT)  |  estrogen receptor & processing

How sensitively the body "hears" estrogen

Worth knowing
ESR1 (rs2234693): CT mixed
ESR1 (rs9340799): AG mixed
CYP1B1 (rs1056836): CG mixed

ESR1 is the gene for the estrogen receptor — think of it as the "antenna" that cells use to hear estrogen's message. CYP1B1 helps break estrogen down afterwards. And from Section 4, COMT sweeps up the leftovers from that breakdown.

Katrina has mixed (heterozygous) versions of the ESR1 and CYP1B1 markers we checked. On their own these are unremarkable — lots of people carry them. But put the three together — the antenna (ESR1), the breakdown (CYP1B1) and the slow clean-up (COMT) — and you get a body that may be especially tuned-in to estrogen's ups and downs. ESR1 variations in particular have been studied in hormone-related migraine.

Note: CYP19A1 (the "aromatase" gene — the very target of the estrogen-lowering medicines in her history) wasn't covered in this data file, so we can't report it.

How this fits her history Katrina's diary is full of lines like "feels like estrogen spiking" and "estrogen going up?", and she logged hundreds of blood-hormone tests. Her hardest years were when estrogen was high and swinging; her steadier years came once it was brought low and stable. The chart below tells that story.

Iron

Genes · TMPRSS6 + HFE  |  iron balance

How the body holds onto iron

Worth knowing
TMPRSS6 (rs855791): AG mixed
HFE C282Y: GG normal
HFE H63D: CC normal

HFE is the gene behind hemochromatosis — a condition of holding onto too much iron. Both of Katrina's HFE markers are the normal version, so there's no sign of iron-overload risk here. That's reassuring.

TMPRSS6 helps fine-tune iron levels. Katrina has a mixed result — one of each version. One of those versions is linked in research to slightly lower iron levels on average. This is a common result and only a gentle nudge, but it's an interesting puzzle piece.

How this fits her history Katrina's diary records many iron infusions and repeatedly low ferritin (an iron-storage measure — noted as low as the teens and 20s). In other words she runs low on iron, not high — and her genes point in exactly that direction: normal HFE (no overload) plus one lower-iron TMPRSS6 copy. Iron is one of the easiest things to keep an eye on with a simple blood test.

Medicines & caffeine

Genes · CYP2C9 · CYP2C19 · VKORC1

How her body processes common medicines

Typical & reassuring

A family of liver genes (the "CYP" genes) decides how quickly the body breaks medicines down. Katrina's results here are reassuringly ordinary:

  • CYP2C9 — normal. No slow-processing variants. This is the gene for anti-inflammatory medicines like celecoxib (Celebrex) and ibuprofen — both of which appear in her history. She processes them at a typical rate.
  • CYP2C19 — normal. No rapid or poor-metabolizer variants found.
  • VKORC1 — normal. If the blood-thinner warfarin were ever needed, she'd be expected to respond to a standard dose.

One gene, CYP2D6, is genuinely hard to read from raw data and its main marker wasn't in this file — so it can't be assessed here.

Caffeine: a fast metabolizer

Just a fun trait

Katrina's CYP1A2 result is the fast version — her body tends to clear caffeine quickly. And at the ADORA2A marker (linked to caffeine jitters and sleep) she has the version less associated with caffeine anxiety. A small but relevant detail: some of the migraine medicines in her history contain caffeine.

Gene · HERC2 / OCA2  |  marker rs12913832

Eye colour — most likely brown, with a real shot at hazel

Trait
Her result: AG one "brown" letter, one "blue" letter

This single SNP is the strongest known eye-colour switch. AA ≈ brown eyes, GG ≈ blue eyes, and AG (Katrina's result) sits in the middle. Most AG people end up with brown or hazel eyes, but green and mixed shades are common, and irises can even appear to "change" with lighting or mood — that's just the AG mix at work. Real eye colour is also shaped by other genes, so this is a strong hint, not a guarantee.

Gene · ABCC11  |  marker rs17822931

Earwax & body odor — the "wet earwax" type

Trait
Her result: CT one "wet" copy, one "dry" copy → still wet

The C allele is dominant and produces the wet, sticky earwax type that's normal across Europe and Africa. Only TT people produce the flaky, dry earwax that's the norm in East Asia. The same gene controls underarm body-odor compounds — so "wet earwax" usually goes with the more typical underarm scent (which is just how most human bodies work; deodorant exists for everyone).

Gene · TAS2R38  |  marker rs713598

Bitter taste — medium taster

Trait
Her result: CG one "taster" copy, one "non-taster" copy

TAS2R38 is the famous "supertaster" gene. Bitter compounds in things like Brussels sprouts, kale, dark chocolate, grapefruit and IPAs are picked up by this receptor. People come in three flavours: GG non-tasters barely notice the bitterness, CC tasters find it strong, and CG mediums (Katrina) sit in between — bitter tastes register but aren't overwhelming. Whether you like bitter foods is a separate, learned story; the gene only sets sensitivity.

Gene · ACTN3  |  marker rs1815739 (the "sprinter gene")

Muscle type — a true all-rounder

Trait
Her result: CT one fast-twitch copy, one endurance copy

ACTN3 helps build fast-twitch ("explosive power") muscle fibres. The C (R577) version produces working ACTN3 protein; the T (X) version produces none. CC people skew toward sprinting and power; TT people skew toward endurance; CT (Katrina) is the mixed all-rounder — well-suited to both quick bursts and longer steady efforts. About one in two people of European ancestry is CT, so this is firmly common, not unusual.

Genes · ALDH2 + ADH1B  |  markers rs671, rs1229984

Alcohol — standard processing, no "flush" reaction

All clear
ALDH2 (flush gene): GG no flush variant
ADH1B (speed): CC standard speed

The "Asian flush" is caused by an ALDH2 variant that makes alcohol's first breakdown product (acetaldehyde) build up — turning the face red and causing nausea even after small amounts. Katrina doesn't carry that variant. Her ADH1B is also the typical European version. Together this just means her body processes alcohol at the regular rate, with no built-in deterrent — which incidentally means there's no genetic safety brake either, so personal moderation is the only governor.

Gene · SLC24A5  |  marker rs1426654

Skin pigmentation — the lighter-skin variant

Trait
Her result: AA two copies of the lighter-skin allele

This is one of the biggest single-gene contributors to skin colour in people of European ancestry. Almost everyone of European descent is AA here. Real skin colour is shaped by many genes together (plus sun exposure), but this is the dominant lighter-skin signal — consistent with a higher chance of sunburn over tan, and the practical advice that goes with it (SPF is a friend).

Gene · FUT2  |  marker rs601338

Norovirus resistance — the lucky "non-secretor" type

Quirk worth knowing
Her result: AA non-secretor (about 1 in 5 people)

FUT2 controls whether the body adds certain sugar molecules to the cells lining the gut. The most common winter "stomach flu" strain (GII.4 norovirus) uses those sugars as a docking station. Non-secretors like Katrina don't make the docking sugars, so they're strongly resistant — many will simply never get norovirus, even when everyone around them is sick. The same gene also nudges blood B12 levels a little higher (which we already saw on her labs) and shifts the gut microbiome slightly. A nice quirk to have!

Gene · DRD2 / ANKK1  |  marker rs1800497 ("Taq1A")

Dopamine signalling — the A1/A1 reward-gene variation

Soft trait
Her result: AA A1/A1 (rarer combination, ~5–10% of people)

This variant sits next to the D2 dopamine receptor gene and is thought to slightly reduce how many of those receptors brain reward circuits carry. People with A1 alleles have been studied a lot in connection with motivation, learning from feedback, risk preference, and addictive behaviours — but the effects are small, mixed across studies, and very dependent on environment. Treat this as a curiosity, not a label.

A small connection Because COMT (Section 4) and DRD2 both live in the same dopamine system, the two together can produce a slightly distinctive profile — a brain that feels things vividly (slow COMT) and weights rewards a bit differently (DRD2 A1). It's one of the more interesting little corners of Katrina's report.
Gene · BDNF  |  marker rs6265 (Val66Met)

Brain "fertilizer" gene — the common Val/Val type

Trait
Her result: CC Val/Val — the common, fully-active form

BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) is a protein that helps brain cells grow and connect — sometimes called "Miracle-Gro for the brain." There are two common versions: Val (fully active) and Met (slightly less effective at being released). Katrina is Val/Val, the most common type, with full BDNF activity. This pairs well with her slow-COMT result — together they tilt toward a brain that processes experience deeply and adapts well to learning and exercise.

Lab · Homocysteine  |  the headline DNA tie-in

Homocysteine: 11.1 µmol/L — up from 9.4

DNA connection

Homocysteine is a tiny molecule the body keeps cleaned up by recycling it — and the recycling line uses MTHFR, folate and B12. A great question to bring to her doctor.

Carrier & rare-condition markers — what the raw data can see

An honest look at what the raw data can tell us about rare inherited conditions — and, just as importantly, what it can't.

23andMe's v5 chip checks around 631,000 specific spots in the genome. A small slice of those spots happens to overlap with well-known mutations behind certain rare diseases. So while this file is not a clinical carrier-screen, we can spot-check a handful of the most famous variants. Here's what comes back for Katrina.

Important reminder. 23andMe raw data is not a medical test. A "no variant detected" result cannot rule out any of these conditions — only a small subset of the disease-causing mutations are checked here. The right tool for real carrier-status screening is a clinical lab (Invitae, Natera Horizon, Sema4, Counsyl, etc.) or, for symptoms, a referral to a medical genetics service. Use this section as a curiosity-driven first look, not a clinical answer.

The honest limit · what the chip simply does not see

Things this data cannot rule out

Important

Even within the conditions above, many disease-causing mutations are not on this chip. Worth knowing:

  • BRCA1/BRCA2: only 3 Ashkenazi founder mutations checked out of thousands.
  • Cystic Fibrosis: the single most common mutation (F508del) is not in this file. Standard clinical CF panels look for 23–200+ variants.
  • Lynch syndrome (hereditary colorectal/uterine cancer — MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2): not covered.
  • Huntington's disease: caused by a CAG-repeat expansion in HTT — SNP chips cannot detect repeat expansions at all.
  • Spinal Muscular Atrophy (SMN1): caused by deletions/copy-number changes — not detectable from SNP chips.
  • Fragile X (FMR1): also a repeat expansion — invisible to this technology.
  • Sickle cell (HBB), Tay-Sachs, Gaucher, Canavan, GJB2 hearing loss: their main variants happened not to be included in this particular file.
  • Prothrombin G20210A: not in file (the other major clotting variant after Factor V Leiden).
  • Familial hypercholesterolemia (LDLR / APOB / PCSK9): the cancer-equivalent for heart disease — clinically important, mostly not covered by this chip.
  • Most rare metabolic, mitochondrial, and neurological conditions: not covered.

When to take this further

If any of the following are true, a proper clinical conversation is worth more than any raw-data scan:

  • Family history of an inherited condition, especially cancers diagnosed young or in multiple relatives.
  • Planning a pregnancy — expanded carrier screening covers 200–500+ recessive conditions and is the appropriate tool.
  • Unexplained symptoms that have a possible genetic flavour — a medical geneticist or a referral to a specialty clinic is the right path.

For each of those, the right tool is a clinical lab carrier screen or a genetic counsellor — not raw 23andMe data. This section is best read as a friendly "nothing common popped up on the spot-checks the chip did happen to test," with the boundaries clearly drawn.

Glossary & full data table

Quick glossary

  • DNA — the four-letter (A,T,C,G) instruction book inside cells.
  • Gene — one "recipe" in that book.
  • Chromosome — a bundle of DNA; humans have 23 pairs.
  • SNP ("snip") — a single spot where people's letters can differ.
  • Allele — one version of a gene or SNP (you have two).
  • Genotype — your specific pair of letters at a spot.
  • Homozygous / Heterozygous — your two letters match / differ.
  • Enzyme — a tiny protein "worker" that speeds up a body process.
  • Polygenic — shaped by many genes together, each with a tiny effect.
  • Variant — a less-common spelling of a SNP.
  • Methylation — a constant background process that helps run DNA and brain chemistry.
