DNA in 2 minutes

Before the genes, here's the whole idea in a few sentences.

DNA is a giant instruction book tucked inside almost every cell of the body. It is written with just four letters — A, T, C and G. A gene is one "recipe" in that book (a recipe for building or running some tiny part of you).

You carry two copies of most genes — one from each parent. A SNP (say it "snip") is a single spot where people's letters can differ — like one word being spelled slightly differently between two copies of the same book. Those tiny spelling differences are what make every person unique, and a handful of them gently nudge how the body works.

Because there are two copies, each result is shown as two letters — one per copy:

  • Homozygous means both letters match (same-same), like A A.
  • Heterozygous means the two letters differ (a mix), like A G.

That's it. The rest of this guide is just translating Katrina's letters into plain English.

The headline gene: COMT

Gene · COMT  |  marker rs4680

The body's "clean-up crew" for stress chemicals

Worth knowing
Her result: AA "Met / Met" — the slow-working version

COMT is an enzyme — a tiny worker — whose job is to clear away certain "fast" chemicals once they've done their job. The chemicals it sweeps up include adrenaline and noradrenaline (the racing-heart, sweaty, "wired" chemicals), dopamine, and the leftover pieces the body makes when it breaks down estrogen.

Katrina has the slow-working version of COMT (both copies read "A", nicknamed Met/Met). About 1 in 4 people have this. It is not a bad thing — people with the slow type often have excellent focus and memory when life is calm. But it means stress chemicals and estrogen leftovers hang around a little longer before they're cleared.

How this fits her history This lines up strikingly with Katrina's own 5.5-year diary. Again and again she describes mornings around 8:30–9:30 with a racing or pounding heart, sweating, feeling "wired," shaky and anxious — almost exactly the pattern you'd expect when adrenaline-type chemicals are slow to switch off. Her genes did not cause those mornings, but the slow COMT may help explain why her body feels them so strongly. Slow COMT is also one of the more consistently studied genes in pain sensitivity and migraine — like a "volume knob" turned up a notch for both stress chemistry and pain.

Folate & the MTHFR gene

Gene · MTHFR  |  markers rs1801133 + rs1801131

A gene that helps the body use folate (vitamin B9)

Worth knowing
C677T: AG one changed copy (heterozygous)
A1298C: GT one changed copy (heterozygous)

MTHFR is a famous little gene. Its job is one specific step in turning the folate you eat (vitamin B9, from leafy greens, beans and so on) into the active form the body actually uses. That active folate powers methylation — a constant background process that helps run DNA, brain chemicals, and the clean-up of a substance called homocysteine.

MTHFR has two very well-known variations, and Katrina carries one copy of each (called "combined heterozygous"). This is common — very roughly 1 in 5 people. The practical effect: her MTHFR enzyme runs at a gentler pace — somewhere around 60–70% of full speed. For most people that matters very little, as long as they get enough folate and B vitamins.

How this fits her history MTHFR is also one of the genes researchers have studied in migraine — especially migraine with aura (the kind with extra sensations like numbness or vertigo, which appear in Katrina's notes). Carrying just one copy of each variant is only a small nudge, not a cause. It's mentioned here because B vitamins (B2/riboflavin, B6, B12 and folate) are an active and hopeful area of migraine research.

Two related "helper" genes — MTR and MTRR, which work alongside MTHFR using vitamin B12 — were also checked. MTR is the common type; MTRR is a mix. Nothing unusual.

The genetics of migraine

This is the part Katrina's family will care about most — so it's worth being very clear and very honest.

Migraine is what scientists call polygenic. That means it is not one gene. It is the combined whisper of 100+ genes, each adding a tiny amount, mixed together with hormones, sleep, stress, weather and more. There is no single "migraine gene" to find.

We looked at four of the most-studied migraine-linked genes — TRPM8, LRP1, PRDM16 and MTDH. Katrina carries the common version at some and the migraine-associated version at others — a completely ordinary mix that millions of people share. None of these "causes" her migraines.

The honest takeaway The genes most meaningfully connected to her picture are the two we already met — COMT (pain and stress chemistry) and MTHFR. But the loudest signal of all isn't in her DNA file at all — it's in her own diary. Across 5.5 years she repeatedly ties her flare-ups to her hormones, and even tracked her blood estrogen against her symptoms. That makes her history the single best "test" anyone could have. The way to read it: genes set the stage; hormones, environment and daily life direct the play.

Hormones & estrogen

Genes · ESR1 + CYP1B1 (+ COMT)  |  estrogen receptor & processing

How sensitively the body "hears" estrogen

Worth knowing
ESR1 (rs2234693): CT mixed
ESR1 (rs9340799): AG mixed
CYP1B1 (rs1056836): CG mixed

ESR1 is the gene for the estrogen receptor — think of it as the "antenna" that cells use to hear estrogen's message. CYP1B1 helps break estrogen down afterwards. And from Section 4, COMT sweeps up the leftovers from that breakdown.

Katrina has mixed (heterozygous) versions of the ESR1 and CYP1B1 markers we checked. On their own these are unremarkable — lots of people carry them. But put the three together — the antenna (ESR1), the breakdown (CYP1B1) and the slow clean-up (COMT) — and you get a body that may be especially tuned-in to estrogen's ups and downs. ESR1 variations in particular have been studied in hormone-related migraine.

Note: CYP19A1 (the "aromatase" gene — the very target of the estrogen-lowering medicines in her history) wasn't covered in this data file, so we can't report it.

How this fits her history Katrina's diary is full of lines like "feels like estrogen spiking" and "estrogen going up?", and she logged hundreds of blood-hormone tests. Her hardest years were when estrogen was high and swinging; her steadier years came once it was brought low and stable. The chart below tells that story.

Iron

Genes · TMPRSS6 + HFE  |  iron balance

How the body holds onto iron

Worth knowing
TMPRSS6 (rs855791): AG mixed
HFE C282Y: GG normal
HFE H63D: CC normal

HFE is the gene behind hemochromatosis — a condition of holding onto too much iron. Both of Katrina's HFE markers are the normal version, so there's no sign of iron-overload risk here. That's reassuring.

TMPRSS6 helps fine-tune iron levels. Katrina has a mixed result — one of each version. One of those versions is linked in research to slightly lower iron levels on average. This is a common result and only a gentle nudge, but it's an interesting puzzle piece.

How this fits her history Katrina's diary records many iron infusions and repeatedly low ferritin (an iron-storage measure — noted as low as the teens and 20s). In other words she runs low on iron, not high — and her genes point in exactly that direction: normal HFE (no overload) plus one lower-iron TMPRSS6 copy. Iron is one of the easiest things to keep an eye on with a simple blood test.

Medicines & caffeine

Genes · CYP2C9 · CYP2C19 · VKORC1

How her body processes common medicines

Typical & reassuring

A family of liver genes (the "CYP" genes) decides how quickly the body breaks medicines down. Katrina's results here are reassuringly ordinary:

  • CYP2C9 — normal. No slow-processing variants. This is the gene for anti-inflammatory medicines like celecoxib (Celebrex) and ibuprofen — both of which appear in her history. She processes them at a typical rate.
  • CYP2C19 — normal. No rapid or poor-metabolizer variants found.
  • VKORC1 — normal. If the blood-thinner warfarin were ever needed, she'd be expected to respond to a standard dose.

One gene, CYP2D6, is genuinely hard to read from raw data and its main marker wasn't in this file — so it can't be assessed here.

Heart & brain — the good news

Gene · APOE  |  markers rs429358 + rs7412

APOE — e3/e3, the most common type

Typical & reassuring

APOE is a gene people often ask about because it's studied in heart health and in Alzheimer's. It comes in three flavours — e2, e3 and e4. Katrina is e3/e3 — the most common pairing (around 6 in 10 people), considered the neutral, baseline result. It is neither the higher-risk e4 nor the e2 type. A genuinely reassuring result.

Typical & reassuring

Lab · Homocysteine  |  the headline DNA tie-in

Homocysteine: 11.1 µmol/L — up from 9.4

DNA connection

Homocysteine is a tiny molecule the body keeps cleaned up by recycling it — and the recycling line uses MTHFR, folate and B12. Katrina's reading is technically "in range" (lab cutoff 14.5), but it has climbed from 9.4 to 11.1 over six months. Many specialists like to see this number under 8–10 for best long-term results.

How her DNA fits This is the clearest gene-meets-lab moment in her whole report. From Section 5, Katrina carries one copy each of MTHFR's C677T and A1298C — her folate-activating enzyme runs at roughly 60–70% speed. Her serum folate (12.1) and B12 (>2000) look fine on paper, but homocysteine creeping up despite that is exactly what a slower MTHFR can produce. The most actionable lever for an MTHFR carrier is usually the form of folate: the methylated form (often labelled L-5-MTHF or "methylfolate") skips the MTHFR step entirely, and the same goes for methyl-B12. A great question to bring to her doctor.

What this guide can't tell you

  • It is not a diagnosis and not a clinical test.
  • Raw 23andMe data can occasionally misread a spot.
  • It does not reliably detect rare inherited conditions — a proper clinical or carrier test does that.
  • It cannot predict the future. Genes are a tendency, not a destiny.
  • A few markers simply weren't in this file (for example the prothrombin clotting marker, CYP2D6, and the aromatase gene CYP19A1).
  • For real ancestry breakdown, the official 23andMe ancestry report does that properly — this guide only touches a couple of trait markers.

Glossary & full data table

Quick glossary

  • DNA — the four-letter (A,T,C,G) instruction book inside cells.
  • Gene — one "recipe" in that book.
  • Chromosome — a bundle of DNA; humans have 23 pairs.
  • SNP ("snip") — a single spot where people's letters can differ.
  • Allele — one version of a gene or SNP (you have two).
  • Genotype — your specific pair of letters at a spot.
  • Homozygous / Heterozygous — your two letters match / differ.
  • Enzyme — a tiny protein "worker" that speeds up a body process.
  • Polygenic — shaped by many genes together, each with a tiny effect.
  • Variant — a less-common spelling of a SNP.
  • Methylation — a constant background process that helps run DNA and brain chemistry.
