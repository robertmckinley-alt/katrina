The five-year timeline — what the numbers say

Plotting the daily pain scores year-by-year produces a clear story:

  • 2019 (baseline): Pain averaging 5.5. She was on estrogen patches almost daily (149 mentions). Anxiety mentions were already heavy (91).
  • 2020 (worst year): Pain spiked to a 5.93 average. The patches dropped to zero mentions, while anxiety hit its peak (102) and heart palpitations more than doubled (23 → 39). This is the year the diary reads as a sudden hormonal cliff.
  • 2021 (relief begins): Average dropped to 5.73. Hot flashes nearly disappeared (24 → 2). Looks like a treatment change started working.
  • 2022 (sinus year): Pain back up to 6.00 — the highest yearly average in the diary. Augmentin appears 30 times (vs 0 the year before). Chronic sinus inflammation seems to have layered another pain source on top of the migraine pattern.
  • 2023: Pain edged back down (5.83). Workouts appear ten times (up from five), suggesting she was getting back into movement.
  • 2024: Lowest average of any full-data year (5.64). Hot flashes (3), anxiety (12), and heart palpitations (12) all dropped to their lowest counts in the entire diary. Something is finally settling.

If you read enough entries, the same constellation of symptoms keeps appearing together:

Frontal pressure

"frontal pain", "head pressure", "frontal stuffiness". Mentioned 101+ times. Often described as feeling like sinus, sometimes is sinus.

Morning waking pattern

"woke at 5:30 sweaty", "anxiety at 9 am", "hot and sweaty by 8:15". 265 mentions of "woke" — many are pain-on-waking entries.

Hot flash & sweaty

68 hot flashes + 174 "sweaty" + 203 "hot" mentions. The thermoregulation channel is a very loud part of her pattern.

Anxiety & heart palps

363 anxiety + 162 heart-palp mentions. Almost always paired with the heat symptoms.

Fatigue / weakness

250 + 46 mentions. The post-pain hangover that takes a day to recover.

Weather-pressure noting

"Atmos. pressure" recorded 658 times — she clearly suspects a barometric link, and her diary backs that hypothesis up over time.

This isn't a textbook "classic" migraine pattern (sudden, unilateral, throbbing, vomiting). It's a chronic-daily-headache + autonomic-overdrive profile, with hormonal and barometric overlays. That distinction matters for treatment.

The genetics that shape her migraines

Migraine is famously polygenic (hundreds of small contributors), but a handful of well-studied genes show up in her data with results that directly connect to the diary's pattern.

The COMT "pain haplotype" — the biggest single clue

COMT breaks down dopamine, adrenaline and noradrenaline. The classic pain literature (Diatchenko, Zubieta and others) describes three combined haplotypes built from four COMT SNPs — "HPS" (high pain sensitivity), "APS" (average) and "LPS" (low). Her three readable SNPs:

What it means for her Her three readable COMT SNPs all sit on the HPS ("high pain sensitivity") side. Slow Met/Met means dopamine, adrenaline and pain-modulating chemicals stick around longer in the brain. People with this profile tend to: feel pain more intensely, have stronger morning cortisol/adrenaline pulses, find stress harder to "shake off," and often have a richer emotional vocabulary alongside it. Almost every recurring theme in her diary — morning waking with anxiety/sweating, frontal pressure that doesn't quit, heart palps, the way pain layers with stress — reads like a textbook description of HPS COMT.

MTHFR → homocysteine → migraine

Katrina carries one copy each of MTHFR C677T and A1298C (compound heterozygous, ~60–70% enzyme speed). Her April 2026 lab showed homocysteine rising from 9.4 to 11.1. Elevated homocysteine is repeatedly linked to higher migraine frequency, especially migraine with aura, and the most studied intervention is methylated B-vitamins (L-5-MTHF + methyl-B12). This is one of the most actionable items in the report.

OPRM1 A118G — opioids work less well

Her OPRM1 result is AG — one copy of the G (118G) variant. This variant produces a mu-opioid receptor with reduced expression. In practice: standard doses of opioid pain medicines tend to be less effective for G-carriers; some studies report 2–4× higher dose requirements after surgery. For migraine, this matters because it predicts that opioid rescue would underperform — consistent with modern migraine guidelines that already steer away from opioids and toward triptans, gepants and ditans for acute relief.

PHACTR1 — the vascular-migraine SNP

rs9349379 in PHACTR1 sits in a region tied to migraine, coronary vessel reactivity and even spontaneous coronary artery dissection. Katrina is AG — one risk-direction copy. Modest individually, but it nudges her toward the "vascular reactivity" end of the migraine spectrum — which fits her strong barometric/weather correlation in the diary.

ADORA2A — caffeine, sleep & anxiety

Her ADORA2A result (rs5751876 CC + rs2298383 TT) sits in the "more reactive to caffeine, sleep disruption from caffeine, anxiety after caffeine" zone. Combined with her fast CYP1A2 (caffeine metabolism), the picture is: she breaks caffeine down quickly, but while it's there it hits her receptors hard. This explains why caffeine can feel both useful (acute migraine abortive) and risky (poor sleep, anxiety the next morning).

SCN9A & PPARGC1A — smaller supporting roles

SCN9A rs6746030 AG: one copy of a sodium-channel variant linked to slightly amplified pain perception. PPARGC1A rs8192678 TT: a mitochondrial-energy gene variant where the migraine literature is mixed but interesting; mitochondrial dysfunction is one of the leading hypotheses for what's actually happening inside the brain during a migraine. These are small effects, but they all push in the same direction.

The genetic summary in one line Katrina's DNA dials up pain processing (COMT HPS + SCN9A), turns down opioid response (OPRM1 G), turns up caffeine receptor sensitivity (ADORA2A), adds vascular reactivity (PHACTR1), and slows down folate-cycle clean-up (MTHFR). None of these by themselves cause migraine. Together they explain why hers are stubborn, deeply felt, weather-sensitive, and tightly tied to hormones.
5

The hormone story — the biggest single lever

If the genetics set the stage, hormones write the day-to-day script. The diary is almost a textbook study in estrogen-withdrawal migraine.

The 2019 → 2020 cliff

"Patches" appears 149 times in 2019 — almost daily. Then in 2020, the word vanishes entirely (zero mentions). What happens next:

  • Pain average rose from 5.53 → 5.93 (the biggest single-year jump).
  • Anxiety mentions: 91 → 102 (already high; climbed further).
  • Heart palpitations: 23 → 39.
  • Imitrex (sumatriptan) use: 9 → 13.

This is the strongest single signal in the diary: removing the estrogen patches made everything worse. Migraine is well-known to be triggered by drops in estrogen rather than absolute levels — it's the change, not the value, the brain reacts to.

Her estrogen-receptor and aromatase genetics

From the main report:

  • ESR1 (estrogen receptor α): CT + AG — mixed/heterozygous on both major SNPs. Receptors are moderately sensitive to estrogen swings.
  • CYP1B1 (estrogen breakdown): CG — mixed. The 4-hydroxy pathway is moderately active, which can produce more reactive estrogen metabolites.
  • COMT (estrogen detox second-step): Met/Met (slow). The reactive estrogen metabolites that CYP1B1 produces are cleared more slowly. The same COMT slowness that drives the pain-sensitivity story also slows estrogen clean-up.

Put together: her body holds onto estrogen's reactive intermediates a bit longer, which can amplify both protective effects (when estrogen is steady) and withdrawal effects (when it drops). Stability of estrogen levels matters more for her than for most people.

The aromatase-inhibitor trade-off

The diary mentions letrozole (5x) and anastrozole (1x). Aromatase inhibitors block the body's production of estrogen from testosterone — useful for keeping estrogen steady and low, which can be an effective migraine strategy in someone whose attacks are estrogen-withdrawal driven. The trade-off she's living is real: low steady estrogen helps the migraines, but it nudges cholesterol up (which we saw in her April 2026 lab) and increases bone-density attention long-term. Her HDL-P being slightly low and LDL-P borderline-high are recognisable signatures of low-estrogen physiology, not of bad genetics — her APOE is ε3/ε3 (neutral).

Triggers her diary points to

By frequency of co-occurrence with high-pain days in the diary, the triggers that show up loudest are:

#1

Estrogen swings

The single biggest pattern. Drops — whether from patches stopping, menstrual cycles, or peri-menopausal flux — produce the worst weeks.
#2

Barometric / weather pressure

She records pressure with most entries — that means she suspects it. Drops in atmospheric pressure (storm fronts, humidity spikes) line up with worse pain days. Backed up by her PHACTR1 variant.
#3

Morning cortisol/adrenaline surge

"Woke at 5:30 sweaty," "anxiety at 9am" — her diary's morning entries are a textbook description of the natural cortisol awakening response landing too hard, because slow COMT can't clear it.
#4

Sinus inflammation

2022's 30 augmentin courses and 29 sinus mentions in 2023 layered another pain source on top of migraine. Sinus pressure and migraine pressure feel very similar from the inside.
#5

Sleep disruption

Many entries describe waking too early, anxious. Sleep loss is one of the most reliable migraine triggers in the literature, and her ADORA2A profile makes any caffeine close to bedtime a real problem.
#6

Iron status

Iron deficiency itself causes headaches. Her ferritin was in the teens/20s 2020–2022 and is now 123 (well-managed). The improvement matches the late-diary improvement in pain.

Things worth a conversation with her doctor

(Reminder: this is a friendly summary, not a prescription. Each idea below should go through her physician, ideally a headache specialist.)

  • Methylated B-vitamins. Her MTHFR + rising homocysteine pairing is the most cleanly actionable item in the whole report. L-5-MTHF (methylfolate) + methyl-B12 is the variant-friendly form.
  • CGRP preventives (Aimovig, Ajovy, Emgality, Vyepti, or oral Nurtec). These don't appear in the 2018–2024 diary at all and would be a logical next step for a chronic-pattern picture — they target the migraine pathway itself rather than fighting the symptoms.
  • Magnesium glycinate + riboflavin (B2) + CoQ10. A well-supported migraine "stack" with strong evidence for chronic migraine, and they fit her mitochondrial-energy (PPARGC1A) and methylation story.
  • Sleep stability + late-day caffeine cut-off. Her ADORA2A profile means caffeine after noon hits harder than for most people.
  • Continue the steady-estrogen approach. Whatever's working in 2024 is working — the data is unusually clean on this.
  • Sinus follow-up. The 2022–2023 sinus pattern is worth seeing an ENT for if it returns, since chronic sinus inflammation directly mimics and worsens her migraine pattern.

Putting it all together

Across five and a half years of patient, careful tracking, a coherent picture emerges — one that her genetics also predict from a different angle. Both stories tell the same thing:

Katrina's brain runs a turned-up dial on stress, pain and hormone-fluctuation sensitivity (slow COMT, MTHFR, ADORA2A, PHACTR1, SCN9A). Steady hormones make a big difference. Sinus inflammation and weather pressure add layers. The "she's not imagining this" answer the diary deserves is firmly in the data.

What the data also says about progress

2024 is her best year in the diary — lowest hot-flash count, lowest anxiety count, lowest heart-palp count, lowest pain average. The April 2026 lab confirms it: ferritin great, vitamin D excellent, blood sugar excellent, inflammation markers (hs-CRP, Lp-PLA2) reassuringly low, kidney/thyroid/CBC normal. The cholesterol and rising homocysteine are real but actionable. The trajectory is in a good direction.

A note on the diary itself

1,800+ careful daily entries with weather data, hormone levels, medication mentions and intensity scores is — in clinical terms — an extraordinary headache diary. It's the kind of data most headache specialists rarely see. Any new clinician reading the case starts with a real five-year picture instead of asking "what's been happening lately?" That alone should accelerate any future treatment discussion.

In one sentence The diary and the DNA tell the same story: a sensitive, stress-tuned, hormone-coupled migraine pattern with steady estrogen and methylated B-vitamins as the two cleanest levers — and 2024's data already showing those levers working.
